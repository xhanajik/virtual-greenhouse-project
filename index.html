Clinical Decision Support Systems - An overview

Based on the results from a systematic review conducted for the Bachelor's thesis :
A Review of Clinical Decision Support Systems Applicable to Precision Oncology (2021)
ABSTRACT:

Background: It has been demonstrated that clinical decision support systems (CDSSs), especially those used in oncology, improve patient outcomes and lower healthcare costs. Nevertheless, despite substantial advancements over the past few decades, CDSSs are often underutilized in routine clinical practice. Furthermore, none of the top commercial oncology CDSSs support active patient participation in treatment decision-making or functional data integration despite both being crucial in a clinical specialty that uses treatments with significant long-term effects on patients.
Objective: To conduct a systematic review to find existing CDSSs applicable to precision oncology and compare their capabilities with the system requirements and expectations set by a clinical oncologist, such as the degree of reflecting individual patient biological profiles and their lifestyle preferences.
Methods: Studies describing eligible CDSS were selected via a systematic review following established guidelines. Earlier, the CDSS requirements were collected using a direct approach in the form of a semi-structured interview with a practicing clinical oncologist and reported using a requirement elicitation and reporting strategy with suitable templates regarding the multidisciplinary nature of CDSS development.
Results: Altogether, the PubMed search yielded 871 articles. Their abstracts were manually screened with 124 full-text articles assessed for eligibility. A total of fifty papers were included in the review, and a total of twelve CDSSs were identified. Additionally, the identified CDSSs were summarized, and subsequently, the results from the requirement elicitation and the review were synthesized. Seven out of the twelve systems provide decision support for a wide range of oncological conditions. The rest propose treatment recommendations only for a selected type of cancer (two for breast cancer, two for prostate cancer, and one for rectal cancer). Additionally, six information storage requirements and four functional requirements are defined.
Conclusion: Despite none of the CDSS fitting all the requirements set by a domain expert, most of the identified systems incorporate a portion of the expected functionality and seem to represent an effort towards a true precision-medicine approach in oncology. Nevertheless, CDSSs are continuously being developed or improved, and the proposed requirements could illustrate a good starting point for developers. Moreover, a participatory design method with the involvement of real-life oncologists and other medical professionals is crucial for creating successful, usable, and appropriate CDSSs.
