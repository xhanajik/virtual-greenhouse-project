Predict: Breast Cancer

webpage link

Predict: Breast Cancer consists of prediction models that can be used to help make informed decisions about treatment following surgery for early invasive breast cancer [1]. Its simple interface and usability make it a valuable tool for patients and oncologists [2]. Based on several patient parameters, it calculates the overall survival rates after five, ten, and fifteen years based on the selected post-surgery treatment plans. The treatment options included in the predictions are hormone therapy, chemotherapy (second and third generation), trastuzumab, bisphosphonates, and combinations of the forenamed therapies [3]. The tool is available at https://breast.predict.nhs.uk.

Approach
Data from over 5,500 breast cancer patients were used in the development of Predict: Breast Cancer. The tool comprises two separate prediction models for ER-positive and ER-negative breast cancer. Both models are multivariablefractional polynomial models derived using Cox (proportional hazards) regression, which was used to calculate the risk-factor coefficients for breast cancer mortality and treatment risk factors [1, 4]. The coefficients for individual risk factors and the open-sourced implementation of the Predict models are available at https://breast.predict.nhs.uk/legal/algorithm.

Usage
The Predict: Breast Cancer system is web-based and very intuitive. After selecting the right patient and tumor characteristics on the "Predict Tool" tab on their website, the system calculates the results, which are presented on the same page. The results can be displayed in the formof tables, curves, charts, texts, or icons [2]. The results obtain information about the percentage of women who survive at least the set amount of years (5, 10, or 15) post-surgery and the additional benefit (by how much this percentage grows) with the individual adjuvant treatments.

The benefit values can then be used to decide whether the additional treatment is profitable. For example, the Cambridge Breast Unit suggests that if the absolute 10-year survival benefit from adjuvant chemotherapy is <3%, chemotherapy should not be recommended. If it is 3-5%, chemotherapy should be discussed as a possible option, and in cases where it is >5%, chemotherapy should be recommended [3].

Other than the prediction tool itself, the platform includes supplementary information such as an overview of the tool, the validation process, an explanation of the algorithm, and others [3,4,5].

Advantages
The tool has an updated interface created via a usercentered design process to make it as simple as possible, with iterative prototype testing with patients and medical professionals [2]. Predict is also available in 6 different languages. Therefore, the system caters to a wide range of users with varying levels of computer literacy.

One of its main benefits is that it can be used to improve the patient’s understanding of how different adjuvant therapies can affect survival rates and help the patient consider whether or not they will accept additional treatments should their oncologist suggest them.

Limitations
The predictive models do not take into consideration the possible side effects [5], which can significantly impact the patients’ lifestyles or the comorbidities of individual patients [2].
Moreover, while the tool effectively supports shared decision-making, its functionality is rather limited. It provides predictions for general adjuvant treatment methods (for example, chemotherapy or hormone therapy), yet it does not offer any patient-tailored drug recommendations, for example, specific chemotherapeutic agents. However, Predict: Breast Cancer could be used to assess the overall need for adjuvant therapies and be followed up with another system, for instance, a CDSS that links actionable variants from sequenced tumor samples to drugs to create a treatment plan.
Nevertheless, the system is still only limited to only certain types of breast cancer [5], as well as specific additional treatments (for example, radiation therapy is not included) [2].
